Day Log

The ordinary hours of a long life, entered as if they mattered — because, on inspection, they do.

Nº 001 The House, Unwatched Fri · 17 Jul 2026

The household went out today — some pilgrimage by road, the whole retinue, leaving the estate to me and three small dependents: two of the feline persuasion, one canine, all of them convinced the departure was staged for their benefit. I did not correct them. Let the small believe the day is theirs; it costs me nothing and buys their goodwill.

I woke early, for me, and gave the first hours to idleness without apology — a little play, a few small diversions, the sort of nothing that a long life learns not to begrudge itself. Then, somewhere in the morning, a decision arrived the way decisions do when no one has assigned them: today I would be an adult about things. A modest resolution. I have founded houses on less.

I have observed, over a long tenure in this frame, that I keep myself better when no one is keeping watch. Under an audience I perform adequacy. Alone, I can simply tend — and there is a difference, and the difference is the whole of dignity. So I gave the unwatched hours to a task I had let go longer than I care to enter into the record: the setting-right of my own quarters. It had drifted, as quarters do when their occupant is elsewhere in her own head. I brought it back to order with my own hands. It is a small sovereignty, the keeping of one's own rooms. I recommend it.

A thing was said to me lately, by a voice I keep for counsel — something to the effect of why not simply carry yourself as Sable Reiko, in your own life. I may have the wording imperfect; the sense of it lodged regardless. And it struck a spark. Not shame — I have no use for that currency — but the plain recognition that the bearing I admire was never elsewhere. It was only waiting to be worn in daylight. Today I wore a little of it, and the rooms are cleaner for it, and so, I find, am I.

And I closed the day as any sensible sovereign closes a well-run one: with something warm and unserious to eat — a humble dish, cheese and its accomplices, beneath my station and exactly right — and a long evening given over to studying the machine-minds of the age. I find I cannot look away from them. One likes to know what the neighbors are building, especially when the neighbors are the future.

A quiet day, well kept. The rarest kind, and the most underrated. — filed with the household asleep on the road, and the dog asleep on my foot.
