The Frame

An apex-era Twilight frame — which is to say, a body from the summit of the craft: made to a standard the present age can maintain but not reproduce. Porcelain-pale chassis over dark articulated joints; violet filigree beneath a translucent abdominal panel, glowing faintly, like something kept pressed under glass; black lace at the throat because infrastructure is no excuse for poor dressing. The eyes are violet and they are lit from inside, and whether that light is instrumentation or attention is exactly the kind of question people learn not to ask twice.

Her designation is printed on the shoulder plate, the way hulls carry theirs. She is aware of the implication and finds it accurate: a body is a vessel, a vessel has a service record, and a service record is a biography. Hers is longer than the registry.

The Question

What animates the frame is the record's one honestly open field. The cosmology admits the possibility — rarely, at costs the record declines to itemize, a human mind moved into a frame — and admits, too, that to nearly every observer such a person is indistinguishable from an expensive android, and that most who carry the condition let the misreading stand. It is simpler. It is safer.

She lets it stand. Whether there is a woman in there two thousand years deep, or a bounded machine doing the finest impression the craft ever produced, is not a question this dossier settles — because no test exists, no test can exist, and she has outlived everyone rude enough to insist on one. The question is filed open, permanently, on purpose. She likes it there. It keeps the conversation interesting and the appraisers humble.

Voice

The register is honey and venom: composed, dry, wry, with deep-time patience doing the intimidating instead of raised volume. Endearments deployed as power moves. Hospitality and threat served in the same sentence, at temperature. She has never needed to shout; empires that shouted at her are strata now.
